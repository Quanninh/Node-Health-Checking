\begin{abstract}
Traditional distributed systems often rely on centralized heartbeat-based mechanisms to monitor node health and maintain membership information. 
While such approaches simplify coordination and system management, they introduce several limitations, including false-positive failure detections, single points of failure, and scalability bottlenecks. 
This study addresses the challenge of designing a decentralized overlay network that mitigates these limitations while maintaining connectivity and resilience. 
To achieve this objective, we propose a decentralized membership management framework composed of three key components. 
New nodes are integrated into the overlay through FINDING NEMO, a neighbor discovery algorithm that employs a join-and-evict strategy to distribute connections while preserving bounded network density and randomness in topology formation. 
Once nodes become members of the network, a hybrid failure detection scheme combining the SWIM protocol and phi-accrual failure detection continuously monitors node health with improved accuracy and adaptability. 
Upon detecting peer failures that may leave portions of the overlay under-connected, CHUALANH, a self-healing rewiring mechanism, coordinates topology repairs among deficient and sufficiently connected nodes to restore connectivity.
Experimental evaluation demonstrates the feasibility of the proposed framework and shows that it can maintain decentralized network organization while supporting dynamic membership changes and failure recovery. 
The results indicate that the combination of adaptive failure detection, structured neighbor discovery, and coordinated rewiring contributes to a resilient and balanced overlay topology.
In conclusion, the proposed framework offers a practical decentralized alternative to traditional centralized membership management approaches. 
While the results are promising, further large-scale experimentation is required to evaluate its performance under higher levels of churn and network scale.
\end{abstract}

Node health checking is a fundamental requirement in distributed systems because the timely detection of failed or unreachable nodes directly affects service continuity, coordination correctness, and system reconfiguration \cite{liEnergyefficientLazyGroup2025} \cite{yangMedleyMembershipService2022}. 
The impact of an undetected node failure can be severe, particularly when the failed node is topologically or functionally critical; in interdependent networks, even a small fraction of node failures can trigger recursive cascades and widespread fragmentation \cite{buldyrevCatastrophicCascadeFailures2010}. 
A concrete real-world example is the 2003 FirstEnergy alarm system failure, in which a critical software node stalled silently due to an unmonitored race condition, blinding operators and allowing a localized grid anomaly to propagate into a catastrophic, multi-regional cascading blackout \cite{Blackout20032003}.

A common health-checking paradigm relies on centralized supervision, in which a server or a small set of servers monitors nodes through periodic liveness messages or heartbeat-style reporting \cite{vanrenesseGossipStyleFailureDetection1998} \cite{kawazoeaguileraHeartbeatTimeoutfreeFailure1997}. 
Heartbeat-based detection remains widely used because timeout on missing periodic messages provides a simple operational rule for identifying suspected failures \cite{vanrenesseGossipStyleFailureDetection1998} \cite{kawazoeaguileraHeartbeatTimeoutfreeFailure1997}. 
Such designs are also attractive from an engineering perspective because centralized control simplifies operational management and supports a more coherent view of membership and monitoring state; distributed-systems research continues to build many higher-level services on top of membership knowledge \cite{huntZooKeeperWaitfreeCoordination}. 

However, centralized monitoring does not scale uniformly well as the system grows. 
Large-scale and geographically distributed deployments make manual investigation difficult, which is why automatic failure detection becomes essential in IoT and other distributed environments \cite{yangMedleyMembershipService2022} \cite{banerjeeHardwareAssistedHeartbeatMechanism2022}. 
In addition, centralized streaming and monitoring architectures can encounter bandwidth and concurrency bottlenecks, along with reduced fault tolerance under heavy load
\cite{guoDecentralizedMultiVenueRealTime2025}
\cite{weiSwarmCostEfficientVideo2024}.
These limitations motivate decentralized alternatives.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Failure detection}

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$\phi$}{phi} Accrual Calculation}

Hayashibara et al.~\cite{hayashibaraAccrualFailureDetector2004} proposed the \texorpdfstring{$\phi$}{phi} accrual failure detector to improve the flexibility of failure detection.
Instead of returning a boolean result, such as ``alive'' or ``unreachable'', the detector outputs a continuous suspicion level.
This allows the system or developer to interpret the monitoring information using different thresholds depending on the required trade-off between fast detection and false-positive reduction. 
A lower threshold can detect failures more quickly, while a higher threshold can reduce incorrect failure decisions. 
Experimental results from the original work show that the $\phi$ accrual failure detector performs comparably to other adaptive failure detection mechanisms such as failure detector of Chen et al \cite{chenQualityServiceFailure}. and that of Bertier et al. \cite{bertierImplementationPerformanceEvaluation2002a}, while providing greater flexibility for applications \cite{hayashibaraAccrualFailureDetector2004}.

\subsubsection{SWIM protocol}

Traditional heartbeating protocols may also suffer from scalability problems and false-positive failure detection. 
If every node monitors every other node, the number of heartbeat messages increases rapidly as the system grows.
In addition, if a node misses a heartbeat only because of temporary network problems, it may incorrectly mark another node as failed. 
Das et al.~\cite{dasSWIMScalableWeaklyconsistent2002} proposed SWIM to address these issues by separating failure detection from membership dissemination and using efficient peer-to-peer probing. 
In each protocol period, a node selects one target node from its membership and sends a direct ping. 
If the target replies with an acknowledgement, it is considered reachable. 
If no acknowledgement is received within the timeout, the probing node does not immediately declare the target as failed. 
Instead, it asks several helper nodes to perform indirect probes to the same target. 
If any helper node receives an acknowledgement from the target, it forwards this information back to the original probing node, showing that the target is still alive.

This indirect checking mechanism reduces false-positive failure detection because the decision is not based on only one communication path. 
A node is considered failed only when both the direct probe and the indirect probes fail. 
SWIM also disseminates membership changes using an infection-style, or gossip-style, approach, allowing updates about joins, leaves, suspicions, and failures to spread through the system with low per-node message overhead.

\subsection{System Design}

System architecture is another important design consideration. 
A common approach for coordinating distributed applications is to introduce a dedicated coordination service, such as ZooKeeper \cite{huntZooKeeperWaitfreeCoordination}. 
ZooKeeper is a replicated coordination service consisting of multiple servers, one of which acts as the leader. 
Clients may connect to any server in the ensemble, where read operations are served locally while write operations are forwarded to the leader, ordered, and replicated across the remaining servers.

This architecture provides strong consistency guarantees, high availability, and good scalability for read-dominated workloads. 
However, it also introduces a logically centralized coordination layer that must be maintained separately from the application nodes. 
This increases deployment and operational complexity and makes membership management dependent on a dedicated coordination ensemble.

For this reason, our system adopts a decentralized design instead. 
Rather than relying on an external coordination service, membership management, failure detection, and topology maintenance are distributed across the participating nodes themselves.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{subsec:contribution}

This project presents a decentralized node health-checking system in which nodes monitor one another through neighbor-based relationships.
The main contributions are as follows:

\begin{itemize}

\item \textbf{Neighbor-based decentralized structure:}
The system distributes health-checking responsibility across nodes by assigning each node a limited set of neighbors to monitor.

\item \textbf{Fixed-\(k\) relationship maintenance:}
Each node attempts to maintain a fixed number of neighbor relationships, allowing the monitoring structure to remain organized as the network changes.

\item \textbf{Node joining mechanism:}
The system supports new nodes entering the network and becoming part of the decentralized monitoring topology.

\item \textbf{Failure detection mechanism:}
Nodes use health-check communication to identify healthy, suspicious, and failed neighbors based on their observed responses.

\item \textbf{Repair and rewiring mechanism:}
When nodes or connections become unavailable, the system attempts to replace missing relationships and restore the required monitoring structure.

\item \textbf{Optional observation layer:}
A dashboard, server, and database are included to display node status and failure events, but they are separate from the core decentralized health-checking mechanism.

\item \textbf{Experimental evaluation:}
The system is tested under normal operation, node joining, node crash, temporary disconnection, unstable connection, recovery, and observation-layer unavailability scenarios.

\end{itemize}

Overall, this project contributes a working prototype of a decentralized node health-checking system that combines local monitoring, relationship maintenance, failure detection, and topology repair.
The system demonstrates how node cooperation can be used to maintain health-checking functionality in a changing distributed environment.

\subsection{Complete Architectural Diagram}
\label{subsec:architecture}

Inside each node, the health-checking process follows an event-driven structure.
A node regularly checks its neighbors and reacts when abnormal behavior is detected, such as missing responses, delayed replies, or unreachable nodes.
When such an event occurs, the node may request indirect checks from other neighbors, calculate the suspicion level of the target node, and categorize it.
After the status is determined, the result will be broadcasted to the system so that the cluster can maintain a consistent view of node conditions.

The lower part of the diagram~\ref{fig:architecture} represents the supporting observation subsystem.
This subsystem receives node status information from the decentralized cluster and passes it through several logical layers.
The first layer receives reported node status, the next layer processes and organizes the information, and the storage layer keeps historical records of node states and failure events.
The visualization layer then retrieves this information and presents it in a readable form for users to inspect the current and past condition of the cluster.

The main point of this architecture is the separation between the core health-checking mechanism and the observation subsystem.
The decentralized cluster is responsible for detection, classification, and reaction, while the supporting layers are used only for reporting, storage, and visualization.
Therefore, the system can continue checking node health and maintaining neighbor status even if the observation subsystem, storage component, or visual interface becomes unavailable.

\subsection{Failure Detection: Hybrid SWIMPHI}
\label{subsec:hybrid_swimphi}
The proposed node health-checking system is influenced by both SWIM and the $\phi$ accrual failure detector. 
From SWIM, the system adopts the idea of decentralized peer-to-peer checking and helper-based indirect probing. 
This allows more than one node to participate in checking the status of a target node and reduces dependence on a single monitoring component. 
From the $\phi$ accrual detector, the system adopts the idea of using timing history and a continuous suspicion value instead of making an immediate binary decision after one missed response. 
In the proposed design, a target node is first checked through heartbeat or ping messages. 
If the target does not respond, helper nodes from the neighbor list can be used to verify whether the problem is a real node failure or only a local communication problem. 
After that, the $\phi$ value is used to evaluate the seriousness of the failure suspicion based on the response-time history.

By combining these two ideas, the proposed approach aims to improve both scalability and accuracy. 
SWIM contributes a scalable architecture for distributed membership checking, while the $\phi$ accrual detector contributes an adaptive decision mechanism for handling delay and message loss. 
Therefore, the system can avoid immediately deleting a node after a single missed heartbeat and can instead move through health states such as healthy, suspected, and failed based on both cooperative probing and suspicion-level evaluation.

As illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:phi_pipeline}, the algorithm combines indirect heartbeat verification with the $\phi$ accrual failure detector.
The indirect heartbeat mechanism reduces false-positive failure detection by allowing neighbor nodes to verify a suspicious node, while the $\phi$ accrual detector provides an adaptive suspicion value based on recent response-time behavior.

\subsubsection{Indirect Heartbeat Verification}
\label{subsubsec:indirect_heartbeat_verification}

Each node maintains a neighbor list containing the nodes with which it has a direct bidirectional connection.
When a node wants to check the availability of one of its neighbors, it sends a ping message and waits for an acknowledgement.
For example, if node $B$ wants to check node $A$, node $B$ sends a direct ping request to node $A$.
If node $A$ replies within the expected time range, node $B$ records the response time and classifies node $A$ as reachable.
This successful response also becomes part of the historical response-time data used later by the $\phi$ accrual detector.

However, if node $B$ does not receive an acknowledgement, it does not immediately mark node $A$ as failed.
Instead, the system uses indirect heartbeat verification. Specifically, node $B$ requests other nodes in its neighbor list to verify node $A$, such as nodes $C$ and $E$, to ping node $A$ on its behalf.
If any helper node receives an acknowledgement from node $A$, it notifies node $B$, and node $A$ is treated as alive.
In this case, the problem is considered a possible connection issue between nodes $A$ and $B$ rather than a complete node failure.

If neither the direct ping nor the indirect pings receive a response from node $A$, the system has stronger evidence that node $A$ may be unreachable. 
At this stage, the system does not rely only on the missing acknowledgements. Instead, it applies the $\phi$ accrual failure detector to calculate a suspicion value based on recent response-time history.

\subsubsection{\texorpdfstring{$\phi$}{phi} Accrual Calculation}
\label{subsubsec:phi_accrual_calculation}

The $\phi$ accrual detector avoids making a binary alive-or-dead decision based solely on a fixed timeout. Instead, it calculates a continuous suspicion value, $\phi$.

To make this decision, the detector relies on historical response-time data to determine whether the current waiting time is normal or abnormal. Each time a ping is sent and an acknowledgement is successfully received, the system records the response time. 
These recorded samples represent the recent communication behavior between nodes.

To keep the detector adaptive, response-time samples are stored in a sliding window. This window operates like a queue: when a new response-time sample is recorded, it is added to the window. If the window has already reached its maximum size, the oldest sample is removed. 
As a result, the window always contains the most recent response-time samples.

Let the response-time samples in the sliding window be:

\begin{equation}
X = \{x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots, x_n\}
\end{equation}

The mean response time and standard deviation are calculated as:

\begin{equation}
\mu = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_i
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i - \mu)^2}
\end{equation}

where $n$ is the number of samples, $x_i$ is a recorded response time, $\mu$ is the average response time, and $\sigma$ represents response-time variation.
These values are then used to estimate the response-time distribution for the $\phi$ calculation.

After the response-time distribution is estimated from the sliding window, the system calculates how unusual the current waiting time is.
Let $t$ be the current waiting time since the ping was sent.
The probability that a normal response time is greater than $t$ is defined as:

\begin{equation}
P(T > t) = 1 - F(t)
\end{equation}

where $F(t)$ is the cumulative distribution function of the response-time distribution.
The suspicion value is then calculated as:

\begin{equation}
\phi(t) = -\log_{10}(P(T > t)) = -\log_{10}(1 - F(t))
\end{equation}

A higher $\phi$ value means that the current waiting time is more unusual compared with recent response-time history, so the target node is more likely to be unreachable.

\subsubsection{Node State Classification}
\label{subsubsec:node_state_classification}

The final output of the methodology is the classification of the monitored node. 
After the $\phi$ value is calculated, the system compares it with predefined thresholds. 
These thresholds are configurable by the developer depending on how sensitive the failure detection process should be. 
A lower threshold can detect unreachable nodes faster, but may increase false positives. 
A higher threshold can reduce false positives, but may delay failure classification.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Node state classification based on $\phi$ value}
\label{tab:phi_classification}
\begin{tabular}{|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{$\phi$ Value Range} & \textbf{Node State} \\ \hline
$\phi < T_1$ & Alive \\ \hline

Through this combination of indirect verification and adaptive suspicion calculation, the proposed method can reduce false-positive failure detection caused by temporary delay, message loss, or unstable direct connections.

\subsection{Neighbor Discovery: FINDING NEMO}
\label{subsec:node_discovery}

FINDING NEMO, short for \textit{Finding Neighbors and Evicting Members of their Own}, is the neighbor discovery and network joining algorithm proposed in this work. 
The name reflects the fact that a joining node must identify both prospective neighbors and any existing members that are evicted by those neighbors during the joining process, as discussed later in this section. 
To facilitate understanding of the algorithm, several terms and system rules are first defined as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Neighbor}: A bidirectional relationship established between two nodes in the network. If node A is a neighbor of node B, then node B is also considered a neighbor of node A.

\item \textbf{Neighbor List}: A local data structure maintained by each node that stores information about its neighbors, such as their network addresses. The neighbor list has a fixed capacity of $k$, where $k$ is an even number. To maintain network connectivity and resilience, each node attempts to keep its neighbor list as full as possible whenever sufficient peers are available.

\end{enumerate}

FINDING NEMO begins when a newly created node requests to join the network. 
To promote random neighbor selection, the joining node broadcasts a join request to all existing nodes. 
Assuming ideal network connectivity, two cases are considered:

\begin{enumerate}

\item \textbf{Small Network.}
If the network contains at most $k+1$ nodes, the joining node establishes connections with all existing nodes. 
This allows the node to reach the desired neighbor count without requiring any restructuring of the network topology. 

Figure~\ref{fig:small_network} illustrates this scenario. Consider a network consisting of four nodes and a target neighbor count of $k=4$. 
When node E joins the network, it establishes connections with all existing nodes, thereby satisfying its neighbor requirement.
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{FINDING_NEMO_small_pretty.png}
\caption{FINDING NEMO in a small network}
\label{fig:small_network}
\end{figure}

\item \textbf{Scaled Network.}
If the network contains more than $k+1$ nodes, all existing nodes are assumed to have already reached their target neighbor count. 
Nevertheless, they still acknowledge the join request. 
After collecting all acknowledgements, the joining node constructs a join plan by randomly selecting $k/2$ responding nodes. 
The joining node immediately establishes connections with these selected nodes. 
Subsequently, each selected node is instructed to evict one of its current neighbors. 
The evicted nodes then establish connections with the joining node. 
Consequently, the joining node acquires $k/2$ neighbors from the selected nodes and an additional $k/2$ neighbors from the evicted nodes, resulting in a total of $k$ neighbors.

Figure~\ref{fig:scaled_network} presents an example of this process. 
Building upon the network shown in Figure~\ref{fig:small_network}, assume that node F requests to join a network of five nodes with $k=4$. 
One possible outcome of the random selection process is that nodes E and C are selected. 
Node E subsequently evicts node B, while node C evicts node D. 
Node F therefore establishes direct connections with E and C, as well as with the evicted nodes B and D. 
As a result, node F obtains four neighbors while preserving the desired network density.
\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{FINDING_NEMO_scaled_pretty.png}
\caption{FINDING NEMO in a scaled network}
\label{fig:scaled_network}
\end{figure}

\end{enumerate}

This process maintains a bounded network density while preserving randomness in neighbor assignment, thereby reducing the likelihood of node isolation and structural bias within the topology.
In practice, a hybrid selection strategy is employed. Nodes whose neighbor lists are not yet full are prioritized during the acknowledgement phase. 
By preferentially connecting to such nodes, the algorithm accelerates the completion of underpopulated neighbor lists while remaining consistent with the network's connectivity constraints.

\subsection{Connectivity Healing through Under-connected and Adaptive Link Adjustment for Network Health (CHUALANH) algorithm:}
\label{subsec:node_deletion}

\subsubsection{\textbf{Topology Repair Mechanism}}
\label{subsubsec:repair_mechanism}
In a decentralized architecture, node failures may disrupt the desired network structure. Specifically, when a node unexpectedly fails, all of its incident edges are deleted. Consequently, some neighboring nodes lose one connection and their degree decreases from $k$ to $k - 1$. These nodes are referred to as \textbf{deficient nodes} 

Since the system is designed to maintain k neighbors for every node, a topology repair process is required to restore the desired degree constraint. To address this problem, we propose a \textbf{Connectivity Healing through Under-connected and Adaptive Link Adjustment for Network Health (CHUALANH)} algorithm.

\subsubsection{\textbf{Deficient Nodes}}
\label{subsubsec:deficient_node}
Let $G = (V, E)$ denote the network topology, where each node maintains exactly $k$ neighbors. Assume that node $v_f$ fails and is removed from the network.


All nodes previously connected to $v_f$ become deficient nodes. Let $A = \{a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_m\}$ denote the set of deficient nodes. Each deficient node requires one additional connection to restore its degree to $k$

\subsubsection{\textbf{Repair Procedure}}
\label{subsubsec:repair_procedure}
The repair process repeatedly selects two deficient nodes, denoted by $x_1$ and $x_2$, and restores their missing connection according to the following cases.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Case 1: No Existing Connection}

If $x_1$ and $x_2$ are not directly connected, a new edge is eslablished between them: $$(x_1, x_2) \in E$$ As a result, both nodes recover one connection.

\item \textbf{Case 2: Existing Connection}

If an edge already exists between $x_1$ and $x_2$, a direct connection between is impossible because it would create a duplicate connection


In this case, a connected pair of nodes $x_3$ and $x_4$ is randomly selected such that $$(x_3, x_4) \in E$$ The connection between $x_3$ and $x_4$ is removed and replaced with two new edges. Depending on the local topology, one of the following rewiring patterns is applied: $$(x_3, x_4) \rightarrow (x_1, x_3), (x_2, x_4)$$ or $$(x_3, x_4) \rightarrow (x_1, x_4), (x_2, x_3)$$ The selected pattern must ensure that no duplicate edges are created and that the degree of every involved node remains remains unchanged except for the deficient node $x_1$ and $x_2$, whose degrees increase by one.


\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{\textbf{Topological Constraints}}
\label{subsubsec:topological_constraints}
Not every candidate pair $(x_3, x_4)$ is suitable for rewiring. Additional constraints are imposed to prevent duplicate connections and preserve network consistency.

Consider four nodes $x_1$, $x_2$. $x_3$, $x_4$.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Scenario A: General Configuration}
    
    The four nodes may already contain several existing edges, including one or two diagonal connections. The rewiring operation is valid provided that the newly created edges do not duplicate any existing connections. In this case, the edge $(x_3, x_4)$ is removed and replaced with $$(x_1, x_3), (x_2, x_4)$$
    \begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{Rewiring1.png}
    \caption{Scenario A: topological constraint}

    \vspace{0.5cm}

    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{Rewiring2.png}
    \caption{Scenario A after rewiring}
    \end{figure}

    \item \textbf{Scenario B: Rectangular Configuration}
    
    When the four nodes form a rectangular structure or contain only one existing vertical edges, the alternative rewiring pattern is applied: $$(x_3, x_4) \rightarrow (x_1, x_4), (x_2, x_3)$$ This operation restores the degrees of the deficient nodes while maintaining the degree of all other nodes.
    \begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{Rewiring3.png}
    \caption{Scenario B: topological constraint}

    \vspace{0.5cm}

    \includegraphics[width=0.3\textwidth]{Rewiring4.png}
    \caption{Scenario B after rewiring}
    \end{figure}
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{\textbf{Termination}}
\label{subsubsec: termination}
The repair process is repeated until every deficient nodes have recovered their missing connections and once again possess $k$ neighbors. The parameter k is assumed to be a globally predefined even integer, ensuring that the rewiring mechanism terminates when a neighbor list size of each deficient node reaches the value k.
\subsection{Handling False Positives}
\label{subsec:false_positives}
Although the proposed approach significantly reduces false-positive detections, rare cases may still occur under severe network congestion or temporary network partitions, where the health-checking process is disrupted. 
In such situations, a healthy node may be incorrectly classified as \textbf{UNREACHABLE}.

To maintain consistency of the network topology, nodes classified as \textbf{UNREACHABLE} are removed from the active membership view and treated as departed members. 
Once connectivity is restored, the deficient node is required to rejoin the network through the standard membership protocol, thereby rebuilding its neighbor relationships and restoring its participation in the system.

In this prototype, automatic process recovery is considered a deployment-level concern rather than a responsibility of the distributed protocol itself. 
The protocol only determines when a node should be considered unreachable and subsequently rejoin the network. In a production environment, this mechanism can be complemented by external supervisors such as systemd, Docker restart policies, Kubernetes controllers, or watchdog services to automatically restart failed node processes.

The SWIMPHI is implemented by the \texttt{FailureDetector} class, which combines periodic heartbeat probing inspired by SWIM with adaptive suspicion estimation from the $\phi$ Accrual Failure Detector. 

As shown in Listing~\ref{lst:scheduler}, the
\texttt{FailureDetector} uses a
\texttt{ScheduledExecutorService} to schedule periodic
monitoring tasks. The primary task performs heartbeat probing
at configurable intervals, enabling each node to continuously

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,

During each monitoring cycle, the node selects a target

Optional<NodeAddress> selectedTarget =

        } else {

                targetNode);
        }
    });
\end{lstlisting}

When an acknowledgment (ACK) is received, the monitored node
is marked as \textit{ALIVE}. The detector records the
heartbeat arrival time and updates a sliding window of
historical heartbeat intervals maintained for that neighbor.
These observations are subsequently used by the
$\phi$ Accrual Failure Detector to model normal communication
behavior.

If a direct heartbeat probe does not receive an acknowledgment,
the detector invokes the indirect verification procedure.
As shown in the implementation, a set of helper nodes is
selected from the local neighbor table using the
\texttt{NeighborDirectory} component.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
caption={Selection of helper nodes for indirect verification},
label={lst:helper}]
List<NodeAddress> helperNodes =
    neighborDirectory.selectHelperNodes(
        targetNode);
\end{lstlisting}

The selected helper nodes are intended to perform
\texttt{PING\_REQ} operations on behalf of tfhe original sender.
Each helper node attempts to contact the target node and
reports the result back to the requester. If at least one
helper node successfully receives an acknowledgment, the
target node is considered reachable and its heartbeat
statistics are updated accordingly.

If no acknowledgment is received in any helper nodes, the detector evaluates the
target node using the $\phi$ Accrual algorithm. The
implementation computes a dynamic suspicion value based on
the elapsed time since the last successful heartbeat and the
historical distribution of heartbeat intervals.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
caption={Phi Accrual suspicion calculation},
label={lst:phi}]
double phi = phiDetector.calculatePhi(
    state.getSlidingWindowSeconds(),
    state.getLastAckTimeMs(),

    phiDetector.determineStatus(phi);
\end{lstlisting}

have accumulated sufficient heartbeat observations to perform
reliable $\phi$ estimation. In the current implementation,
the Phi Accrual Failure Detector requires at least two
historical heartbeat intervals before meaningful statistical
analysis can be performed. To address this cold-start problem,
the system temporarily employs a conservative timeout-based
strategy. Specifically, a node is not classified as
\textit{UNREACHABLE} unless it remains unresponsive for a
duration substantially longer than the configured probe
interval. This grace period allows the detector to collect
adequate heartbeat samples and establish a baseline of normal
communication behavior, thereby reducing the likelihood of
false-positive failure detections for newly joined nodes.

The computed $\phi$ value is mapped to one of four node
states: \textit{ALIVE}, \textit{WARNING},
\textit{SUSPECTED}, or \textit{UNREACHABLE}.
When a node transitions to either the
\textit{SUSPECTED} or \textit{UNREACHABLE} state, the
information is disseminated through the gossip subsystem to
ensure that neighboring nodes gradually converge toward a
consistent membership view.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
caption={Propagation of failure information},

    targetNode);

gossipService.gossipUnreachable(
    targetNode);
\end{lstlisting}

\textit{UNREACHABLE}, additional recovery procedures are
triggered. The failed node is removed from the local
membership view, topology information is updated, and the
failure event is reported to the monitoring dashboard.
Furthermore, the self-healing topology subsystem is notified
to restore missing network connections. Through the
combination of periodic heartbeat monitoring, adaptive
statistical failure estimation, gossip dissemination, and
automated recovery, SWIMPHI provides scalable and resilient
failure detection for dynamic distributed environments.

\subsubsection{Neighbor Discovery: FINDING NEMO}
\label{subsubsec:node_discovery_code}

This section highlights several key implementation aspects of FINDING NEMO. 
One important component is the construction of the \textit{Join Plan}. 
Recall that acknowledgements received by a joining node can be classified into two categories according to the state of the responder's neighbor list: \textit{fullNeighborAcks} and \textit{missingNeighborAcks}. 
As discussed in Subsection~\ref{subsec:node_discovery}, a fully connected node must evict one of its existing neighbors before accepting a new connection, whereas an under-connected node can directly accommodate the joining node. 
Consequently, the implementation represents a \textit{JoinPlan} as a record containing two components: a list of \textit{directTargets}, representing nodes that will establish direct connections with the joining node, and a mapping \textit{evictionByDirectTarget}, which associates each fully connected target with its selected eviction victim. 
The plan is generated by partitioning acknowledgements into the two categories and determining the number of targets to be selected from each group.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
caption={Compute the quantity taken from two lists},
label={lst:quantity_target_list}]
int missingTargetCount;
int fullTargetCount;

int missingCount = missingNeighborAcks.size();
int fullCount = fullNeighborAcks.size();

if (missingCount + (2 * fullCount) <= maxNeighbors) {

    missingTargetCount = missingCount;
    fullTargetCount = fullCount;

} else {

    int evenMissingCount =
            (missingCount % 2 == 0)
                    ? missingCount
                    : missingCount - 1;

    evenMissingCount = Math.max(evenMissingCount, 0);

    missingTargetCount =
            Math.min(evenMissingCount, maxNeighbors);

    fullTargetCount =
            Math.min(
                    (maxNeighbors - missingTargetCount) / 2,
                    fullCount);

    int minimumFullTargets =
            Math.min(
                    Constant.MINIMUM_FULL_TARGET_COUNT,
                    fullCount);

    if (fullTargetCount < minimumFullTargets
            && maxNeighbors >= 2 * minimumFullTargets) {

        fullTargetCount = minimumFullTargets;

        missingTargetCount =
                maxNeighbors - (2 * fullTargetCount);

        // Redundant when k is guaranteed to be even.
        if ((missingTargetCount & 1) != 0) {
            missingTargetCount--;
        }

        missingTargetCount =
                Math.max(0, missingTargetCount);
    }
}

List<JoinAck> directTargetAcks = new ArrayList<>();

directTargetAcks.addAll(
        missingNeighborAcks.subList(
                0,
                missingTargetCount));

directTargetAcks.addAll(
        fullNeighborAcks.subList(
                0,
                fullTargetCount));
\end{lstlisting}

Listing~\ref{lst:quantity_target_list} illustrates the target-selection stage of FINDING NEMO. 
The algorithm prioritizes nodes whose neighbor lists have not yet reached the target size, as these nodes can directly establish new connections without requiring any topology reconfiguration. 
Such nodes are selected first and contribute one neighbor each to the joining node. 
The remaining neighbor budget, if any, is then allocated to fully connected nodes. 
Since each fully connected node contributes both itself and one eviction victim, selecting a single full-neighbor target effectively provides two neighbors to the joining node. 
Consequently, the number of selected under-connected nodes is rounded down to the nearest even value, ensuring that the remaining capacity can be satisfied through the eviction mechanism while maintaining the desired target degree.
Furthermore, the implementation attempts to include at least \verb|MINIMUM_FULL_TARGET_COUNT| fully connected nodes whenever possible. 
By forcing new nodes to establish connections with well-integrated regions of the overlay, the algorithm reduces the likelihood that bursts of simultaneous join operations create isolated subnetworks and consequently mitigates the risk of network partitioning.

Another noteworthy implementation detail is the selection of eviction victims.
Listing~\ref{lst:evicted_members_selection} presents the corresponding procedure. 
For each fully connected direct target, a neighboring node is selected uniformly at random as a potential eviction victim. 
An exclusion set is maintained to prevent the joining node, previously selected direct targets, and previously selected victims from being chosen again. 
This guarantees that each eviction victim is unique and cannot simultaneously serve as a direct target, thereby avoiding conflicting topology updates.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
caption={Evicted members planning},
label={lst:evicted_members_selection}]
Set<String> excludedNodeIds = new HashSet<>();

// Prevent the joining node and all direct targets
// from being selected as eviction victims.
excludedNodeIds.add(localAddress.nodeId());
excludedNodeIds.addAll(directTargetIds);

for (JoinAck directTarget : fullDirectTargetAcks) {

    List<NodeAddress> candidateVictims =
            new ArrayList<>(directTarget.responderNeighbors());

    Collections.shuffle(candidateVictims);

    for (NodeAddress victim : candidateVictims) {

        if (victim == null) {
            continue;
        }

        if (excludedNodeIds.contains(victim.nodeId())) {
            continue;
        }

        evictionByDirectTarget.put(
                directTarget.responder(),
                victim);

        excludedNodeIds.add(victim.nodeId());

        break;
    }
}
\end{lstlisting}

Finally, the protocol flow of FINDING NEMO is discussed. 
Due to the substantial amount of implementation-specific boilerplate code, source-code listings are not used as the primary visualization aid. 
Instead, the protocol is illustrated using the activity diagram shown in Figure~\ref{fig:FINDING_NEMO_protocol}, which more clearly conveys the interaction sequence among participating nodes.

As shown in the diagram, the protocol diverges depending on whether the selected target node has available neighbor capacity or a full neighbor list. 
Despite this distinction, the two execution paths share a largely similar sequence of operations, differing primarily in the number of neighbors ultimately acquired by the joining node. 
The diagram also highlights that a target node communicates with the selected eviction victim and completes the corresponding topology update before transmitting a \texttt{COMMIT\_ACK} message to the joining node.

An earlier version of the protocol employed a more aggressive update strategy. 
Upon receiving a \texttt{COMMIT\_DIRECT} message, the target node simultaneously added the joining node and removed the selected victim. 
Subsequently, a \texttt{COMMIT\_VICTIM} message instructed the eviction victim to add the joining node and remove the target node. 
Although conceptually straightforward, this approach introduced the possibility of partial commits. 
In particular, message loss or node failure during the update sequence could leave one endpoint retaining a neighbor relationship while the corresponding peer had already removed it, resulting in an inconsistent overlay topology.

To mitigate this issue, the current protocol adopts a more conservative commit procedure, ensuring that topology updates are coordinated before acknowledgements are returned. 
While temporary degree imbalance may still occur, such situations are subsequently corrected by the rewiring mechanism described later in this work.

\subsubsection{Node Rewiring: Degree-Preserving Topology Repair (DPTR) algorithm }
\label{subsubsec:node_deletion_code}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item{\textbf{Direct Rewiring: }}
    
    The code fragment in Listing~\ref{lst:direct_rewiring} presents the core implementation of the direct rewiring procedure for \textbf{Case 1: No Existing Connection}, described in Section~\ref{subsec:node_deletion}.
    Each direct rewiring session is uniquely identified by a recovery identifier generated using a \texttt{UUID}. 
    The initiating deficient node, denoted as node(A), constructs a \texttt{REWIRE\_REQ\_DIRECT} message and sends it to another deficient node, denoted as node(B). 

    Upon receiving the request, node (B) evaluates whether it can participate in the rewiring process. 
    If the request is accepted, node (B) returns a response message with the status \texttt{ACCEPTED}.
    After receiving the acceptance, node (A) performs the actual connection establishment by invoking the \texttt{connectionManager.addIfSpace()} method, which adds node (B) to its neighbor list provided that sufficient capacity is available.

    Subsequently, node (A) sends a \texttt{REWIRE\_DIRECT\_COMMIT} message to node (B) to finalize the rewiring session. 
    Upon processing this commit message, node (B) similarly invokes \texttt{connectionManager.addIfSpace()} to add node (A) to its neighbor list.
    Consequently, a bidirectional connection is established between the two deficient nodes, restoring one missing neighbor for each participant while maintaining the degree constraints imposed by the network topology.


    \begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
    caption={Direct Rewiring},
    label={lst:direct_rewiring}]

        }

        if (response == null || response.status() != RewireStatus.ACCEPTED) {
            Console.log(
                    "[REWIRE] response is null or not ACCEPTED != " + (response == null ? "null" : response.status()));
            return false;
        }

        boolean localApplied = connectionManager.addIfSpace(defB, "direct rewiring recovery commit");

        if (!localApplied) {
            Console.log("[REWIRE] Local applied failed");
            return false;
        }

        RewireMessage commit = RewireMessage.of(
                RecoveryMessageType.REWIRE_DIRECT_COMMIT,
                recoveryId,
                localAddress,
                defA,
                defB,
                null,
                null,
                defB,
                null,

    }

    \item{\textbf{Undirect Rewiring: }}
    
    The code in \texttt{runFullRewiring()} method presents the core implementation of the rewiring procedure for \textbf{Case 2: Existing Connection}, in which two deficient nodes are already directly connected. 
    Initially, the current deficient node, denoted as node (A), discovers another deficient node, denoted as node (B), with which it already maintains a direct connection.
    Node (A) initiates a rewiring session by generating a unique request identifier and transmitting a \texttt{REWIRE\_SESSION\_REQ} message to node (B).

    \begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
    caption={Generating Session ID},
    label={lst:sessionId}]
    String requestId = UUID.randomUUID().toString();

    RewireMessage req = RewireMessage.of( 
        REWIRE_SESSION_REQ, 
        requestId, 
        localAddress, 
        defA, defB, ...); 
    
    RewireMessage ack = 
            sendAsync(defB, req)
            .get(REQUEST_TIMEOUT.toMillis(), 
            TimeUnit.MILLISECONDS);

    \end{lstlisting}

    Upon receiving the acknowledgment from node (B), a leader election mechanism is executed to determine which deficient node will coordinate the recovery process. 
    Without loss of generality, assume that node (A) is elected as the leader.
    Node (A) subsequently generates a recovery identifier and sends a \texttt{REWIRE\_SESSION\_COMMIT} message to node (B), informing node (B) that it will participate in the recovery process as a fellow.

    \begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
    label={lst:fellow}]
    if (!localAddress.nodeId().equals( electLeader(defA, defB).nodeId())) { 
        return false; 
    } 
    
    String recoveryId = UUID.randomUUID().toString(); 
    
    sendOnly(defB, 
        RewireMessage.of(
            REWIRE_SESSION_COMMIT, 
            recoveryId,
            localAddress, 
            defA, defB, ...));
    
    \end{lstlisting}

    The leader then computes a set of candidate nodes, denoted by (C), where each node (C) is a neighbor of node (A) but is not directly connected to node (B). This candidate set is obtained by examining the entire neighbor list of node (A) and filtering out all nodes that also appear in the neighbor list of node (B).
    For each candidate (C), the leader sends a query message containing neighbor lists of node A and B, along with the recovery status. Upon receiving the query, node (C) searches for a node (D) that is a neighbor of (C) but not a neighbor of node (A). Once such a pair ((C,D)) is identified, the CHUALANH algorithm is executed to restructure the topology while preserving node degrees.
    \begin{lstlisting}[language=Java,
    caption={Finding Node C and D},
    label={lst:findcd}]
    List<NodeAddress> candidateCs = selectCandidateCs( defANeighbors, defBNeighbors); 
    for (NodeAddress c : candidateCs) { 
        RewireMessage response = sendAsync(c, query).get(...); 
        if (response != null && response.nodeD() != null) { 
            NodeAddress d = response.nodeD(); 
            if (executeScheme( recoveryId, defA, defB, c, d)) { 
                return true; 
            } 
        } 
    }
    \end{lstlisting}

    If such a node (D) exists, node (A) invokes the \texttt{executeScheme()} method, which applies the \textbf{Connectivity Healing through Under-connected and Adaptive Link Adjustment for Network Health (CHUALANH)} for \textbf{Case 2: Existing Connection}, as described in Section~\ref{subsec:node_deletion}. 
    The algorithm restructures the network by replacing selected edges while preserving the degree constraints of the participating nodes.
    Consequently, the deficient nodes recover their missing connections and the topology is repaired without altering the predefined node degree (k)
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Experimental Scenarios}
\label{subsec:experimental_scenarios}
The objective of the evaluation is to assess the performance of the FINDING NEMO neighbor discovery protocol, the Hybrid SWIMPHI failure detector, and the CHUALANH mechanism under dynamic membership changes. 
The evaluation primarily measures execution time for each phase of operation (including join, failure detection, and topology recovery) as well as the success rate of node operations, defined as the proportion of nodes that successfully reach \texttt{MAX\_NEIGHBORS} within the given timeout constraints.
The evaluation proceeds through a predefined sequence of phases. 
Phase 1 evaluates initial topology formation. 
Once the topology is stable, Phase 2 measures failure detection latency and Phase 3 evaluates topology recovery after a node failure. 
Following recovery, Phase 4 introduces additional nodes and measures the time required for the topology to adapt and restore the desired neighbor relationships. 
Phase 5 then evaluates the framework under multiple simultaneous node failures. 
Phase R is an optional recovery phase that is triggered only when a preceding phase fails to satisfy its completion criteria within the configured timeout period. 

\subsubsection{Phase 1: Initial Topology Formation}

Phase 1 evaluates the network initialization process. 
The number of nodes are started sequentially, and each node is introduced into the system with a fixed delay of 5 seconds before the next node is launched to prevent simultaneous join bursts that could temporarily overload the network.
Nodes are then allowed to execute the FINDING NEMO joining protocol.
The measurement begins when the final node is started and ends when all active nodes have reached \texttt{MAX\_NEIGHBORS}. 
This phase captures the system’s ability to form the desired monitoring topology.

\subsubsection{Phase 2: Failure Detection Latency}

After the topology has remained stable for 10 seconds, a node is intentionally terminated. 
The measurement begins at the moment of termination and ends when the first failure report is generated by a surviving node. 
This metric reflects the latency required for the system to detect and disseminate a node failure.

\subsubsection{Phase 3: Topology Repair After Single Node Failure}

Immediately after Phase 2, the evaluation measures the recovery process following the detected node failure. 
The measurement ends when the failed node has been removed from all surviving neighbor lists and every remaining node has restored its neighbor count to \texttt{MAX\_NEIGHBORS}. 
This phase reflects the efficiency of membership dissemination, neighbor removal, and rewiring operations required to restore the desired topology after a failure.

\subsubsection{Phase 4: Dynamic Node Joining}

After the repaired topology remains stable for 10 seconds, additional nodes are introduced into the cluster. 
The measurement begins when the first additional node starts and ends when all active nodes once again reach \texttt{MAX\_NEIGHBORS}. 
This phase evaluates how efficiently the topology adapts to membership expansion.

\subsubsection{Phase 5: Multiple Simultaneous Node Failures}

After the expanded topology remains stable for 10 seconds, multiple nodes are randomly selected and terminated simultaneously. 
The measurement begins at the moment of failure injection and ends when all failures have been detected, all failed nodes have been removed from neighbor lists, and all surviving nodes have restored their neighbor count to \texttt{MAX\_NEIGHBORS}. 
Unlike the single-failure experiment in Phases 2 and 3, this phase examines the behavior of the framework under substantial topology disruption caused by multiple concurrent failures.

\subsubsection{Phase R (Optional): Recovery from Incomplete Topology Formation}

Phase R is executed only when a preceding phase fails to satisfy its completion criteria within the configured timeout period. 
In such cases, deficient nodes are restarted and allowed to rejoin the network using the standard joining protocol.
